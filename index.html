SeeBelow: Sub-dermal 3D Reconstruction of Tumors with Surgical Robotic Palpation and Tactile Exploration

Raghava Uppuluri, Abhinaba Bhattacharjee, Sohel Anwar, Yu She
Purdue University
Video Code

Abstract

Surgical scene understanding in Robot assisted Minimally Invasive Surgery (RMIS) is highly reliant on visual cues lacking tactile perception of the surgical anatomy. Haptic feedback based optimal force modulated surgical palpations are necessary for localization, geometry or depth estimation and dexterous exploration of mini-tumors, lumps and abnormal stiff inclusions of underlying subsurface tissue layers. Prior works explored surface level tissue abnormalities or single layered tissue-tumor embeddings with >300 palpations for dense 2D stiffness mapping. Our approach focuses on 3D reconstructions of sub-dermal tumor surface profile in multi- tissue-layered (skin-fat-muscle) 3D printed tumor embeddings using a visually guided novel tactile navigation policy. A robotic palpation probe with custom designed palpation end-effector comprising external tri-axial force sensing was leveraged for tactile exploration of the phantom. The exploration policy initiates with a surgical scene registration engaging surgeon- in-the-loop tumor annotated region of interest (ROI) selection on depth camera generated phantom surface point clouds. The specified surgical scene is transformed into a interpolated surface grid map to optimally locate tumor coordinates using Bayesian Optimization based probing for stiffness mapping. Detected tumor coordinates are further explored with a tumor contour following contact-safe impedance controller to trace the sub-dermal tumor geometry across their depths reaching the underlying tumor-tissue boundary. Projections of these contour following palpation trajectories allows 3D reconstruction of the subdermal tumor surface profile in less than 100 palpations. Our approach ensures high fidelity geometry estimations and 3D surface reconstructions of rigid tumor embeddings in tissue layers with isotropic elasticities, although soft tumor geometries with variable an-isotropic elasticities are yet to be explored.
